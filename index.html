01 · The Paper

A J-shape, not a line

Mortality risk doesn't rise smoothly with glucose. It bottoms out in a narrow band (80–94 mg/dL) and climbs on both sides — low glucose carries risk too.

◉
80–94 mg/dL

Optimal range

The nadir of the J-curve, consistent across every age and sex group. Authors propose 100 mg/dL — not the older 110 — as the true upper limit of normoglycemia.

▲
+10–32% per 18 mg/dL

Hyperglycemia risk

Each 18 mg/dL above optimal multiplies mortality. The effect is strongest at ages 35–44 and attenuates with age, but never disappears.

▼
< 80 mg/dL

Hypoglycemia arm

Below 80 mg/dL, mortality rises sharply — especially under 70. Critical context for people on insulin, sulfonylureas, or those tracking CGM lows.

⚥
Men > Women until ~73 yr

Sex & age divergence

Women's glucose rises sharply around menopause (~age 50). The male–female gap peaks at age 48–51 then closes; after ~73 the lines cross.

04 · Methodology & Limitations

How to read these numbers

What is a hazard ratio?

A hazard ratio (HR) of 1.50 means a 50% higher rate of death over the study period, compared to the reference group. HRs describe populations, not individuals — your personal outcome depends on many factors this study did not measure.

The cohort

12,455,361 Korean adults from the National Health Insurance Service (NHIS), 2001–2013. Mean BMI 23.5 kg/m² — leaner than typical Western cohorts. Self-reported diabetes was excluded. Cause-specific mortality and HbA1c were not available.

Adjustments

Cox models stratified by age and sex, adjusted for BMI, smoking, alcohol, physical activity, blood pressure, lipids, and household income. Reference band: 90–94 mg/dL. HRs reported per 18 mg/dL (1 mmol/L) increment.

Limitations

Single fasting measurement underestimates true effect (regression dilution). Korean population may not generalize to other ethnic groups. The hypoglycemia arm is approximated from Figure 2 since Table 2 does not stratify HRs below 80 mg/dL. Causation is not established.

Citation

Yi, S.W., Park, S., Lee, Y.H. et al. Association between fasting glucose and all-cause mortality according to sex and age: prospective cohort study. Scientific Reports 7, 8194 (2017). https://doi.org/10.1038/s41598-017-08498-6
